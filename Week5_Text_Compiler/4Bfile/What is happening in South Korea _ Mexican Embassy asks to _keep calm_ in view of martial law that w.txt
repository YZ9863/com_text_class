       <p>South Korean President Yoon Suk Yeol surprised the country by declaring "emergency martial law". As reported by AP News, Yoon justified his decision as a measure to restore order in the country.</p><p>The measure, announced Tuesday night, was quickly rejected by the parliament, although the military insists that it will remain in force until the president revokes it.</p><h2>The presidential argument: to eradicate "pro-North Korean" forces.</h2><p>In a televised conference, Yoon Suk Yeol justified his decision to invoke Martial Law, for the first time in South Korea in 45 years, on the grounds that he seeks to "protect the democratic constitutional order" against what he described as internal threats.</p><p>The president argued that the opposition: controls parliament, sympathizes with North Korea and paralyzes the government with "unpatriotic" actions.</p><p>"I will rebuild and protect the free Republic of Korea, which is falling into the depths of national ruin," the president declared.</p><p>You might also be interested in: South Korea declares emergency martial law</p><p>However, the measure generated strong criticism both from the opposition and within his own party.</p><p>Han Dong-hoon, leader of the ruling party, called the martial law "wrong" and promised to work to reverse it.</p><p>For his part, Lee Jae-myung, opposition leader, called it "illegal and unconstitutional."</p><h2>What does martial law entail?</h2>
<p>The declaration of martial law gives the military broad authority to assume civilian responsibilities, which could limit individual freedoms and disrupt the functioning of parliament.</p><p>It places restrictions on political activities, such as prohibiting protests and strikes that could "incite social disorder." It also allows arrests and seizures without warrants.</p><h3>The decree includes six main orders, according to the Washington Post:</h3><ul><li>Ban on all political activities, including those of the parliament.</li><li>Restrictions on the press under military control.</li><li>Prohibition of strikes, work stoppages and protests that incite social disorder.</li><li>Striking doctors to return to work within 48 hours or face punishment.</li><li>Measures against "false propaganda" and manipulation of public opinion.</li><li>"Good" citizens will have minimal measures of discomfort, excluding "anti-national forces."</li></ul><p>Yoon asked the public to "tolerate some discomfort" and trust his leadership during this period.</p><h2>Parliament's response: Unanimous vote of 190/190 in the early morning.</h2><p>The National Assembly reacted quickly to Yoon's proclamation.
